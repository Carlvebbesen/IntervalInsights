# Recovery & Adaptation

> **Fitness is built during recovery, not during the workout.** Adaptation needs adequate rest, sleep, and fuel.

## Rationale

- Training is the stimulus; the body supercompensates *afterward*, given recovery.
- Without it, stress accumulates into non-functional overreaching and eventually overtraining.

## How to apply

- Pair hard days with easy/rest days; protect `[[sleep]]` and refuel after sessions
  (`[[post-training-nutrition]]`). Eat enough across the week — chronic under-fuelling
  (`[[energy-availability]]`) silently blocks adaptation.
- Don't blunt the adaptation you trained for: skip high-dose `[[antioxidants]]` supplements around
  hard blocks.
- Honor down weeks (see `[[periodization]]`), `[[tapering|taper]]` into key races, and the easy 80% of `[[polarized-80-20]]`.
- Watch monitoring signals: resting HR, HRV, mood, sleep quality, persistent soreness. Quantified
  load (`[[acute-training-load|ATL]]` / `[[training-stress-balance|TSB]]`) is a coarse complement —
  e.g. TSB below ~−20 for weeks *with* falling workout quality flags a real need to back off.

## The muscular dimension

- In running, the slowest thing to recover is usually the **muscle**, not the heart or hormones
  (`[[muscular-load-limits-training]]`). Accumulated `[[muscle-tone]]` is a leading cause of
  persistent "heavy legs."
- This is why sub-threshold work and the `[[double-threshold]]` structure recover so well: the
  full inflammatory/DOMS response (peaks 24–72 h) lands on the *following* easy day, so you get
  *"much more training without much more recovery cost."*

## Violations to flag

> [!warning] Consecutive hard days with no easy/rest day, chronically poor sleep alongside
> rising load, or ignoring elevated resting HR / falling HRV / accumulated muscle tone — all
> undermine adaptation.
