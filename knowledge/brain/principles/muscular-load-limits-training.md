# The Muscular System Limits Training Load

> In distance running the ceiling on how much **quality** you can absorb is the **muscular
> system** — not the heart or hormones. Weigh every session's *effect* against its *muscular cost*.

## Rationale

- Cardiovascular and endocrine systems recover quickly; **muscle** is what accumulates damage
  (DOMS peaks 24–72 h), tension, and stiffness ([[muscle-tone]]).
- Sub-threshold work delivers a large aerobic stimulus at **low muscular cost**, and clustering
  it into [[double-threshold]] days spends the muscular budget efficiently — *"much more
  training without much more recovery cost."*
- *"The limiting factor in terms of load comes (mostly) down to the muscular system in running…
  fill as much as possible of the 'correct' training while balancing the muscular system."*

## How to apply

- Prefer the **lowest intensity that still drives the target adaptation** (threshold, not race
  pace, for the base).
- Use **intervals over continuous** efforts — same threshold work at lower tone.
- **Cluster** hard work into fewer days, with full easy days between to let tone fall.
- Actively manage [[muscle-tone]] (easy days, massage, not over-stiffening with strength at the
  wrong time — schedule hypertrophy / [[muscle-growth]] work so it doesn't tax the legs before quality).

## Violations to flag

> [!warning] Piling on intensity or race-pace work for a marginal *mechanical* gain while
> wrecking the legs; back-to-back hard days with no easy day between; judging readiness only by
> HR, feel, or a single load number (`[[training-stress-score|TSS]]` / `[[acute-training-load|ATL]]`)
> while ignoring accumulated muscular load — *"not all TSS is created equal,"* and load metrics
> are blind to muscle (`[[performance-management-chart]]`).
