# Specificity

> Adaptations are **specific** to the stimulus — you get better at what you actually practice.

## Rationale

- Training adaptations target the systems, movement patterns, and energy demands you load.
- The closer training resembles the goal event (pace, terrain, duration, modality), the
  more it transfers.

## How to apply

- As the goal approaches (`[[periodization]]`), bias sessions toward race demands:
  goal pace, goal duration, goal terrain — sharpened on the `[[tapering|taper]]`.
- Early phases can be more general; specificity rises over the plan via `[[progressive-overload]]`.

## House default: "specific" base work = the threshold, not race pace

> [!note] This brain's default (`[[norwegian-method]]`)
> The Bakken/Norwegian view — which this brain follows — pushes back on a naïve reading of specificity: *"a common flaw is
> thinking the most specific way of training is doing work at race pace."* Because mechanical
> speed is ultimately capped by the **anaerobic threshold**, the most *physiologically* specific
> base work is optimizing that threshold (`[[lactate-threshold]]`, `[[double-threshold]]`) plus
> total volume — **not** large amounts of race-pace running, which costs more muscularly for less
> aerobic return (`[[muscular-load-limits-training]]`). Race-pace specificity still matters, but
> it is layered on *late* and sparingly. Reconcile by phase: threshold-specific in the base,
> race-pace-specific near the event.

## Violations to flag

> [!warning] A plan whose key sessions never resemble the target event (e.g. only short
> intervals when training for a marathon) violates specificity — but so does a plan that is
> *all* race-pace work year-round with no threshold base.
