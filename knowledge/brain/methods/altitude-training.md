# Altitude Training

> Training at **moderate altitude (≈2000–2600 m)** to drive oxygen-carrying adaptations, then
> timing the **descent** so threshold speed peaks for racing.

## How it works

- **Kenyan model:** live and train at 2000–2600 m year-round; use **"high–low"** — drop to
  ~1000 m (often a nearby valley) for speed work in the weeks before racing.
- **Bakken model (short blocks):** **7–10 day** camps with [[double-threshold]] on days 1, 3,
  5, 7, then a "post-altitude cycle" of easy work + threshold preservation. Longer **3–6 week**
  stays also work but are more traditional/phased.
- **Post-altitude peaking window:** individual but **repeatable** for a given athlete — usually
  **day 1–3 OR day 11–18** after coming down. Plan key races into that window.
- **Summer return (7–10 days, mid-season)** re-elevates threshold while preserving race
  sharpness; even **5–10 day** stays give a measurable threshold boost.

## Physiology

- Hypoxia stimulates red-blood-cell / hemoglobin (EPO-mediated) gains; on descent the improved
  O₂ transport shows up as **higher threshold speed** (and supports [[vo2max]]).
- Elite Kenyan blood profile (per [[src-evertsen-kenyan-physiology]]): **RBC mass ~35 ml/kg**,
  Hb **14–18 g/dl** (normal), normal EPO, **high hemoglobin–oxygen affinity** — i.e. efficient
  transport rather than abnormal blood values. Their edge is **genetics + intense training at
  moderate altitude**, where work near LT (≈90 % VO₂max) is especially effective.

## When to use it

- As the main source of **variation** inside an otherwise flat, threshold-dominant block
  ([[norwegian-method]]); slot the peaking window into [[periodization]] before key races.
  Norwegian Olympic practice leans on the same systematic, high-volume altitude work
  (`[[src-olt-endurance-development-pod]]`).

## Caveats

- Altitude lowers attainable running speed — adjust paces and lean on lactate, not the clock.
- The response (and the peaking window) is highly individual; test and repeat what works.
- **Iron must be replete before a camp:** the haemoglobin gains depend on iron for red-cell
  synthesis — check status first ([[iron-status]]; general sports-science, not in the source).
