# The Norwegian Method

> Lactate-controlled, **threshold-dominant** training that clusters quality work (above all
> the [[double-threshold]] day) to maximize sustainable threshold development at the lowest
> possible muscular cost.

Developed empirically by [[marius-bakken]] (5,500+ in-field lactate tests; 5000 m PB 13:06),
refined with coaches Frank Evertsen, Leif Olav Alnes and others, and later carried to the
Ingebrigtsen brothers via Eric Toogood. Now used across distance running and endurance sport
(Jakob Ingebrigtsen; triathletes Kristian Blummenfelt & Gustav Iden).

> [!note] House default
> This is the brain's **default training model** — the coach plans and evaluates from here unless
> told otherwise. Strict polarized (`[[polarized-training]]`) is the documented alternative. See
> `[[polarized-80-20]]`.

## How it works

1. **Control intensity by measurement, not feel** ([[control-intensity-by-measurement]], [[lactate-testing]]).
   Train threshold in a low, sustainable **sweet spot of ~2–3 mmol/L** (Bakken ~2.3–3.0;
   world-class Kenyans as low as 2.0) — *below* the textbook 4.0 mmol/L, which came from
   studies on non-elite athletes.
2. **Make threshold the main dish.** Most quality is sub-threshold ([[lactate-threshold]]),
   done as **intervals (30 s–10 min), not continuous** — intervals reach a higher threshold
   speed at lower muscular wear.
3. **Cluster it into [[double-threshold]] days** (AM longer reps + PM shorter reps, ~6–8 h
   apart), **2×/week (Tue/Thu)**, separated by full easy days.
4. **Keep easy easy.** ≥~80 % of ~180 km/wk at **<1.0 mmol/L, <70 % HRmax**. Deliberately
   avoid the "no-man's-land" between easy and threshold.
5. **One weekly "X element"** — hills ([[hill-reps]]) or short fast reps at **5–8 mmol/L** —
   for a stimulus above threshold without flat-running wear.
6. **Use [[altitude-training]]** (short 7–10 day blocks + post-altitude peaking) as the main
   source of variation.
7. **Respect the real limiter — the muscular system** ([[muscular-load-limits-training]],
   [[muscle-tone]]) — not the heart or hormones.

## Typical winter week (Bakken 2004, ~180 km, at altitude)

| Day | AM | PM |
|-----|----|----|
| Mon | easy | easy |
| **Tue** | **6 × 2000 m @ 2–3 mmol/L** | **20–25 × 400 m @ ~3.0 mmol/L** |
| Wed | easy | easy |
| **Thu** | **4 × 10 min @ 2–3 mmol/L** ([[10min-threshold-reps]]) | **10–12 × 1000 m @ ~3.0 mmol/L** ([[1000m-reps]]) |
| Fri | easy | easy |
| Sat | hills / 10 × 200 m in 26–28 s | — |
| Sun | long run ≤ 16–18 km | — |

Two double-threshold days, one "X element," the rest easy. ~13 runs/week.

## Intensity distribution: pyramidal, not strictly polarized

The model is usually classed as **threshold / pyramidal**, not polarized. It agrees with
[[polarized-80-20]] that easy work dominates (~80 % by volume) and that the truly "grey"
zone is wasteful — but it deliberately concentrates the *hard* budget **at controlled
threshold** rather than at VO₂max. See the `> [!warning] Contested` note on [[polarized-80-20]].

## When to use it

- Trained distance runners with a large [[aerobic-base]] who can support high mileage and
  have a way to control intensity (lactate meter ideal; otherwise careful HR/talk-test).
- Base/build phases (`[[periodization]]`); shifts toward race-specific work in summer.

## Evidence & caveats

- Explicitly **empirical** ("thoughts and experiences," not a controlled study), but backed by
  5,500+ lactate tests and the NTNU 2024 split-session study (see [[double-threshold]]).
- Demands intensity control and a big base; intensity values are individual and need lab
  confirmation at the top level.
