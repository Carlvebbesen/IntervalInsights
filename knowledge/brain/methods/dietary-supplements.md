# Dietary Supplements

> **Food first.** Supplements can't replace a varied diet and only help when correcting a real
> **deficiency** or as one of the few evidence-backed ergogenic aids — and they carry a **doping
> risk**. How to think about what to take.

## How it works — per `[[src-olt-supplements]]`

- **Routine, evidence-based for most athletes:** **fish oil / omega-3 (~5 ml/day, year-round)** and
  **vitamin D**. Iron and calcium **only on documented need** (`[[iron-status]]`, `[[bone-health]]`).
- **Ergogenic aids with documented (individual) effect:** **caffeine**, **creatine** (~30%
  non-responders; long-term effects "not known"), and pre-session buffering with `[[bicarbonate]]`.
- **Skip:** amino-acid supplements (no documented effect — `[[protein-for-athletes]]`); high-dose
  `[[antioxidants]]` (can be counterproductive); routine `[[probiotics]]` for healthy athletes.

## When to use it

- After the diet (`[[performance-nutrition]]`, `[[meal-pattern]]`) is right and a need is confirmed —
  never as a shortcut around fuelling or `[[energy-availability]]`.

## Evidence & caveats

> [!warning] Doping & contamination risk
> A supplement can trigger a **positive doping test** from a substance **not on the label**. Use
> only products vetted against Olympiatoppen's **risk assessment** ("risikovurdering") /
> Antidoping Norge. Large doses can be toxic and **impair absorption** of other nutrients.
