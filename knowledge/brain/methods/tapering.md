# Tapering

> The pre-race **"formtopping"**: shed accumulated fatigue by cutting training volume while
> *keeping* intensity, so fitness is preserved but freshness rises. The peak step of
> `[[periodization]]`.

## How it works

- **Cut volume, keep intensity and frequency.** Drop weekly volume by roughly **40–60%** over
  **~7–14 days**, but keep short, sharp efforts at race intensity so you don't detrain.
- **Progressive (not step) taper** generally wins: ramp volume down gradually/exponentially into
  the race.
- **Freshness mechanism:** fatigue clears faster than fitness fades — accumulated `[[muscle-tone]]`
  and glycogen/hormonal stress resolve while `[[vo2max]]` and threshold hold, producing
  supercompensation timed to the start line (`[[recovery-and-adaptation]]`).
- **Pair with the nutrition taper:** race-week `[[glycogen-loading]]` is the fuel arm of the same window.
- **On the chart:** a taper is fatigue (`[[acute-training-load|ATL]]`) falling faster than fitness
  (`[[chronic-training-load|CTL]]`), so **form** (`[[training-stress-balance|TSB]]`) rises into the
  race — the `[[performance-management-chart]]` view of formtopping. The corollary: *don't* do a big
  week just before a race; that work mostly delays freshness rather than adding race-day fitness.

## When to use it

- Into every **key race**. Slot it as the Peak phase of `[[periodization]]`; this is also where
  late race-pace `[[specificity]]` work is sharpened (low volume, race intensity).

## Evidence & caveats

- Meta-analyses put the typical gain around **~2–3%** (endurance-science consensus — Mujika &
  Padilla; Bosquet et al. — **not** stated in the OLT source, which only names *formtopping* as part
  of the model).
- **The classic mistake is cutting intensity**, which detrains — keep the hard bits, lose the bulk.
- Taper length is individual — bigger engines / higher-volume athletes often need longer; test and repeat.
