# Strength for Endurance

> Heavy and explosive strength work that improves **`[[running-economy]]`** and durability —
> *without* chasing the size of `[[muscle-growth]]`. For endurance the goal is **neural** (force
> per stride), not bulk.

## How it works

- **Heavy, low-volume:** ~**2×/week**, compound lifts (squat, deadlift, hip/calf work) at **3–6
  reps**, a few sets, full recovery — train **rate of force development**, not hypertrophy.
- **Or plyometric/explosive:** jumps, bounding, short steep `[[hill-reps]]` for stiffness and
  reactive strength.
- **Goal ≠ mass:** done this way it adds little body weight — distinct from `[[muscle-growth]]`,
  which needs an energy surplus and higher reps.

## When to use it

- Year-round, **2×/week**, reduced into a `[[tapering|taper]]`. **Timing matters most:** put heavy
  lifting **on or after quality/hard run days** (or easy days), and **never in the ~24 h before a
  key threshold/quality run** — respect `[[muscular-load-limits-training]]` and don't spike
  `[[muscle-tone]]` before the runs that matter.

## Evidence & caveats

- Heavy strength improves running economy by **~2–8%** and delays fatigue (strength-training
  consensus — Rønnestad & Mujika; Beattie et al. — **not** in the OLT source, which states only
  that for endurance athletes strength gives "the same mechanistic benefits" but must be balanced
  against aerobic capacity and recovery).
- **Concurrent-training interference** is real when lifting and hard aerobic work pile up
  under-recovered — separate them and protect the run quality.
- Also supports `[[bone-health]]`.
