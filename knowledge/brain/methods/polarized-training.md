# Polarized Training

> A training-intensity distribution that concentrates work at the **extremes** — lots of easy,
> some very hard (toward VO₂max), little in between. In this brain it is the **alternative** to
> the default `[[norwegian-method]]`, kept for reference and specific use.

## How it works

1. Bulk of training (~80%) in Z1–Z2 of `[[training-zones]]` to build `[[aerobic-base]]`.
2. A smaller share (~20%) at **high intensity (Z4–Z5)**, e.g. `[[4x4-norwegian]]`.
3. Deliberately **minimize Z3** ("the middle"), which adds fatigue without proportional benefit.

This is the *practical implementation* of strict polarized; both it and the threshold model are
~80% easy and avoid the uncontrolled grey zone (see `[[polarized-80-20]]`).

## Default vs. alternative (this brain)

> [!note] Not the house default
> This brain follows the **threshold / pyramidal `[[norwegian-method]]`** as its default — the
> hard ~20% sits at controlled sub-threshold work, justified by
> `[[muscular-load-limits-training]]`, with VO₂max work as a small sharpening element. Polarized
> here is the documented alternative: reach for it when an athlete/event genuinely needs a
> VO₂max-led block, or to contrast against the default.

## When to use it (as the alternative)

- Short, sharp build phases where raising `[[vo2max]]` is the explicit bottleneck.
- Athletes who respond poorly to high-frequency threshold load and need fewer, harder sessions.

## Evidence & caveats

- Backed by descriptive studies of elite athletes and several trials (`[[src-seiler-2010]]`).
- The threshold-focused model has an equally strong basis (`[[src-bakken-norwegian-model]]`) and
  is this brain's default. Neither is a universal law — but here, threshold-first wins ties.
