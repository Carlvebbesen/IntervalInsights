# Double Threshold Training

> Two controlled threshold sessions on the **same day** (~6–8 h apart), reframed as *one long
> session with a functional rest break in the middle* — the core engine of the [[norwegian-method]].

> [!tip] The mindset shift
> "I stopped thinking of two threshold sessions on a single day as two separate workouts and
> started thinking of them as one long session with a functional rest break in the middle."
> — [[marius-bakken]]

## How it works

- **Morning:** longer reps (2000–3000 m, or **6 × 10 min** — see [[10min-threshold-reps]]) at
  the **low end of threshold, 2–3 mmol/L**. Goal: big aerobic stimulus while keeping
  [[muscle-tone]] moderated. A drift above 3 mmol/L by the last rep means it was too fast.
- **Gap: 6–8 h.** Shorter loses some of the benefit; beyond ~8–10 h it becomes two separate
  workouts and the cumulative effect breaks down.
- **Evening:** shorter, faster reps (400–1000 m, e.g. [[1000m-reps]]) **still capped at 2–3 mmol/L**,
  typically **15–20 s/km faster** than the morning. Do **not** add dose because it feels easy —
  the receptive feeling is the signal to stay disciplined.
- **Both as intervals with recoveries** (lowers tone within the session, keeps lactate in
  range, makes the work repeatable); continuous running makes this very hard to hold.
- **Frequency:** 2 double-days/week (Tue/Thu), with **≥1 full easy day between** to let
  accumulated tone fall.

## Why it works — three pillars

1. **Training-load limit.** Sub-threshold intensity keeps hormonal *and* muscular stress low.
   You work inside the **receptive window before** the full inflammatory/DOMS response (which
   peaks 24–72 h later); the body reads the day as one stimulus and the full cost lands the
   next day, which is easy. → *"much more training without much more recovery cost."*
2. **Performance limit.** Raising **LT2 / [[fractional-utilization]]** (and [[running-economy]])
   beats chasing [[vo2max]] once it has plateaued — and threshold is where you recruit the
   right fibers at the lowest relative cost.
3. **Adaptation & specificity.** Threshold pace sits just below race pace, so you train
   race-relevant motor units and movement patterns **aerobically, repeatedly, in volume**.

## NTNU 2024 study (the supporting evidence)

14 well-trained athletes (VO₂max 69.2), same 6 × 10 min volume done two ways:

| | One long 6 × 10 min | Split 3 × 10 (AM) + 3 × 10 (PM), 6.5 h apart |
|---|---|---|
| HR, 2nd vs 1st half | **+2.8 %** (drift up, ~168→173) | **−2.5 %** (171→166, *dropped*) |
| Lactate, 2nd vs 1st | **+0.41 mmol/L** | **−0.58 mmol/L** |
| Next-day fatigue / soreness | 7.0 / 6.0 | **8.0 / 7.0** (better recovery) |

Same volume, **substantially lower physiological cost when split.** (Glycogen drawdown from the
AM run also tends to make PM lactate read *lower* at the same effort.)

## Lactate / intensity

- Target **2–3 mmol/L** (golden zone **2.3–3.0**), ≈ **80–87 % HRmax** for well-trained runners.
- Evening lactate usually reads *lower* than morning at the same effort.

## When to use it

- Trained runners with a large [[aerobic-base]] and a way to control intensity.
- Buildup/winter: run the PM session in partial glycogen depletion for extra adaptation.
  Race season: **refuel aggressively** after the AM run (`[[carbohydrate-fueling]]`,
  `[[fuel-for-the-work-required]]`) and shorten the sessions.

## Caveats

- Needs intensity control; **be conservative when signals conflict** (see
  [[control-intensity-by-measurement]]). Built on top of a big easy-volume base.
