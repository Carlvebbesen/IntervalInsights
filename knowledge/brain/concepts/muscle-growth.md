# Muscle Growth

> Hypertrophy is driven by **resistance training**, supported by a modest energy surplus and
> adequate `[[protein-for-athletes]]`. You can't reliably gain muscle and lose fat at the same time.

## Why it matters

- For endurance athletes, **strength work** (`[[strength-for-endurance]]`) supports economy, injury resistance, and `[[bone-health]]`
  — but it must be balanced against aerobic capacity and the muscular recovery budget
  (`[[muscular-load-limits-training]]`, `[[muscle-tone]]`).

## Key facts — per `[[src-olt-muscle-growth]]`

- **Resistance training is decisive:** without it, only ~30–40% of weight gained is muscle; **with**
  regular strength training, **75–100%** is muscle.
- **Energy surplus: +250–500 kcal/day.** **Protein: 1.2–2 g/kg/day** — beyond **2 g/kg** adds no
  extra hypertrophy.
- **Realistic rate:** untrained up to **~20%/year**; trained athletes only **~1–3%/year**.
- **Post-session:** **30–50 g carbohydrate + 10–20 g protein within 30 min** (carbs → insulin →
  anabolic/anti-catabolic). Protein powder is unnecessary with adequate energy (`[[dietary-supplements]]`).

> [!note] Concurrent training
> Building mass needs a surplus; endurance performance often needs leanness — these pull in
> opposite directions. Periodise them, and never chase muscle into a state of low
> `[[energy-availability]]`. (Creatine helps some, but ~30% are non-responders.)
