# Running Economy

> The oxygen (energy) cost of running at a given speed — once [[vo2max]] has plateaued, the
> main lever left for getting faster.

## Why it matters

- Two runners with the **same [[vo2max]]** race very differently if one is more economical, or
  can hold a higher % of VO₂max at threshold ([[fractional-utilization]]).
- A signature edge of Kenyan runners and of long-career improvers like Paula Radcliffe.

## Key facts

- **Radcliffe (Jones 2006):** O₂ cost at 16 km/h fell **205 → 175 ml/kg/km (~15 %)** from 1992
  to 2003 (later ~165), while VO₂max stayed ~70 ml/kg/min. The performance gains came from
  **economy + threshold speed, not a higher VO₂max.**
- Elite Kenyan 5000 m runners: highly developed economy, high running velocity at 2 / 4 / 7
  mmol/L lactate, high capillary density, and many/large mitochondria (per
  [[src-evertsen-kenyan-physiology]]).
- Improved by lots of running, sustained threshold work, and a neuromuscular stimulus
  (strides, [[hill-reps]], [[strength-for-endurance|strength]]) — partly why the [[norwegian-method]] keeps a weekly "X
  element."
