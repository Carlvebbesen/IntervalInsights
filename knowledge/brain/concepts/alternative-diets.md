# Alternative Diets & Performance

> Popular restrictive diets (low-carb/keto, Zone, Atkins, strict low-GI) **underperform for
> endurance athletes** because they under-deliver the carbohydrate and energy hard training needs.

## Why it matters

- The brain's house view, reinforced here: carbohydrate is the fuel of moderate-to-high intensity
  work (`[[carbohydrate-fueling]]`), and matching intake to demand is a guardrail
  (`[[fuel-for-the-work-required]]`). Restrictive diets break both — and often induce inadvertent
  low `[[energy-availability]]`.

## Key facts — per `[[src-olt-alternative-diets]]`

- **Zone (40/30/30):** worked example — a 60 kg female athlete training 2+ h/day gets only
  **~1,735 kcal/day** and **~2.9 g carb/kg**, "far less carbohydrate than an athlete needs."
- **Atkins / keto:** "nearly impossible to train with so little carbohydrate"; side effects incl.
  headache, dehydration, arrhythmia, kidney problems.
- **Strict low-GI:** healthy in principle but **insufficient carbohydrate** for hard, high-intensity
  training.
- **Bottom line:** without enough carbohydrate to refill glycogen, optimal moderate-to-high
  intensity performance over duration is **impossible**.

> [!warning] Coach guardrail
> A self-imposed low-carb/restrictive diet on a hard endurance program is a red flag for junk
> sessions, blunted adaptation, and LEA. Evidence-based `[[performance-nutrition]]` beats fad diets.
