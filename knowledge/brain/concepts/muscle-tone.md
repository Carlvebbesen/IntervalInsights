# Muscle Tone

> The baseline tension in resting muscle — in the [[norwegian-method]], the single thing that
> most limits how much quality training you can actually absorb.

## Why it matters

- Bakken's central claim: the limiter of training **load** in distance running is the muscular
  system, not the heart or hormones ([[muscular-load-limits-training]]). Manage tone → train
  more, with less risk.
- *"Roughly 90 % of runners who complain of persistent 'heavy legs' have accumulated excessive
  muscle tone."*

## Key facts

- Tone has **neural** (motor-neuron activity) and **non-neural** components (titin, passive
  sarcomere tension, fiber viscoelasticity, the extracellular matrix, local biochemistry).
- **Sub-threshold work raises tone far less** than harder efforts, and tone recovers faster
  relative to the load — which is *why* [[double-threshold]] works: the PM session benefits
  from the optimal tone left by the AM session, and tone falls fast on the easy day after.
- Session design follows tone: **longer reps in the morning** (keep tone moderated), **shorter,
  faster reps in the evening**; the AM↔PM pace gap is ~15–20 s/km.
- Bakken observed **~20 s/km** threshold-pace swings between periods of well- vs poorly-managed
  tone. **Cross-friction massage** lowers tone (his 2009 master's thesis). Trained athletes can
  *palpate* tone changing across a day.
