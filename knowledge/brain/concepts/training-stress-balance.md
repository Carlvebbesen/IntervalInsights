# Training Stress Balance (TSB) — "Form"

> **TSB = `[[chronic-training-load|CTL]]` − `[[acute-training-load|ATL]]`** — fitness minus
> fatigue — i.e. **how fresh you are relative to your fitness**. Positive = fresh, negative =
> carrying fatigue.

## Why it matters

- Form is what you actually race on: enough fitness banked, fatigue shed at the right moment. It
  turns `[[tapering]]` and `[[periodization]]` into something you can read on a chart.
- A taper works precisely because **"CTL falls, but because TSB/freshness rises, the decline in
  fitness is outmatched over the short term by an increase in freshness"** (`[[src-trainerroad-tss-pmc]]`).

## TSB ranges (rough guides, not hard rules)

| TSB | Meaning |
|-----|---------|
| **+10 to +25** | well rested, good form — ideal for racing or hard efforts |
| **0 to −10** | normal training; some fatigue while building fitness |
| **−10 to −30** | heavy training block — productive but challenging |
| **below −30** | overreaching risk — monitor closely |

## Key facts

- **The number is a rough approximation, not fact** — both sources warn against treating TSB as
  objective. Read it with perceived effort and recovery (`[[performance-management-chart]]`).
- **Optimal race-day form is individual:** some athletes need TSB highly positive, others race best
  only mildly positive (varies with age, experience, event type, training volume).
- **Form needs fitness behind it:** a positive TSB sitting on a low `[[chronic-training-load|CTL]]`
  is just rest, not readiness — "positive form is only useful if you have built the CTL to back it up."
- **Warning sign:** TSB below ~**−20** for several weeks **with** declining workout quality signals
  a real need for recovery (`[[recovery-and-adaptation]]`); don't pile on a big week before a race.
