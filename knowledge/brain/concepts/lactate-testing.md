# Lactate Testing

> Measuring blood lactate **during training** (not just in a lab) to set and police intensity
> precisely — the instrument that makes the [[norwegian-method]] / [[kenyan-training]]
> threshold model repeatable.

## Why it matters

- Lactate at a given pace is **consistent day-to-day and year-to-year**; heart rate drifts with
  sleep, fuel, heat and fatigue. So lactate is the most reliable control (see
  [[control-intensity-by-measurement]]).
- *"Feeling comfortable / hard is simply not good enough, if you want to be really good. You
  need to KNOW."*

## Key facts

- **In-field testing:** a fingertip meter (e.g. Aekray, Lactate Scout) sampled after intervals.
  Bakken ran **5,500+ tests** over his career.
- **Typical targets:** sub-threshold quality **2–3 mmol/L**; easy **<1.0**; track sessions
  **5–8** (race season **7–10+**). See [[training-zones]].
- **The "traffic-light" read** (Bakken, on a treadmill):
  - 🟢 **Green** — low warm-up HR + low lactate at high speed + high HR at threshold speed →
    *extend* the session.
  - 🟡 **Yellow** — normal response → standard session.
  - 🔴 **Red** — high warm-up HR, can't raise HR at the target lactate, no improvement by rep
    2–3 → *end it early*.
- **The lactate curve is a fitness gauge:** a **flat** lactate curve at a held pace = good form;
  a **steep rise** at the same pace = declining form / overreaching.
- After ~5–6 sessions, trained athletes **internalize** the target pace and need the meter less.
- A **treadmill** standardizes speed/surface/conditions so sessions are comparable.
