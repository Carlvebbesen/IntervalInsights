# Lactate Threshold

> The exercise intensity at which blood lactate begins to accumulate faster than it can be cleared.

## Why it matters

- Marks the boundary between "comfortably sustainable" and "racing the clock."
- The second threshold (LT2 / MLSS) defines Z4 in `[[training-zones]]` and is a strong
  predictor of endurance race performance — often more so than `[[vo2max]]` in trained athletes
  (see `[[fractional-utilization]]`).
- It is the **primary training target** of the `[[norwegian-method]]` and `[[kenyan-training]]`.

## Key facts

- Two thresholds are commonly used: **LT1** (aerobic, lactate first rises above baseline)
  and **LT2** (anaerobic / maximal lactate steady state). Sustainable race pace tracks LT2.
- Raised by a large `[[aerobic-base]]`, targeted threshold work, and `[[altitude-training]]`.
- Efforts **at/above LT2** are limited partly by **acidosis (H⁺)**, not lactate itself; pre-loading
  `[[bicarbonate]]` can buffer it for hard, short work.

## The sub-threshold "sweet spot"

- The textbook **4.0 mmol/L** threshold value came from studies on *non-elite* athletes. Elite
  practice trains a **lower, controlled band of ~2–3 mmol/L** — Bakken ~**2.3–3.0**, world-class
  Kenyans as low as **2.0** (`[[src-bakken-norwegian-model]]`).
- Working *just below* LT2 gives most of the adaptation at a fraction of the muscular cost,
  which is what makes high-frequency `[[double-threshold]]` work sustainable.
- **Individual & level-dependent:** Kenyan long-distance LT ≈ **3.0** (range 3–4), middle-distance
  ≈ **4.5** mmol/L; the better the athlete, the *lower* the lab lactate value tends to sit even as
  speed/power rises. Control it by measurement, not the textbook number (`[[lactate-testing]]`,
  `[[control-intensity-by-measurement]]`).
