# Energy Availability

> The energy left for the body's basic physiology **after exercise burns its share** — dietary
> energy minus exercise energy expenditure, relative to fat-free mass. Chronically too low is the
> root failure mode behind many "mystery" performance and health declines.

## Why it matters

- Low energy availability (LEA) is the engine of **RED-S / the Female Athlete Triad**: menstrual
  dysfunction, low estrogen, and **bone loss** (`[[bone-health]]`), plus impaired iron status
  (`[[iron-status]]`), recovery, immunity, and adaptation (`[[recovery-and-adaptation]]`).
- It is a **fuelling** problem, not a training problem — you can train perfectly and still decline
  if you don't eat enough for the load. This is the chronic, whole-diet companion to the per-session
  `[[fuel-for-the-work-required]]` guardrail.

## Key facts

- Per `[[src-olt-bone-health]]`: LEA triggers menstrual dysfunction and bone loss **independent of
  age**; amenorrhea **≥4–6 months** warrants medical follow-up.
- The commonly cited clinical threshold is **~30 kcal/kg fat-free mass per day** (below ≈ "low",
  optimal ≈ 45) — *IOC RED-S / Loucks consensus, **not** stated in the OLT source.*
- Restrictive `[[alternative-diets]]` are a common cause of inadvertent LEA in endurance athletes.

> [!warning] A guardrail the coach must respect
> Rising training load with flat or falling intake = LEA risk. Signs: lost menstrual cycle,
> stalled progress, frequent illness/injury, stress fracture, low ferritin. **Fix energy intake
> before adding training.** Don't prescribe volume into a deficit (`[[meal-pattern]]`).
