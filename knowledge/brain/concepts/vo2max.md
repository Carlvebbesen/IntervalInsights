# VO₂max

> The maximum rate at which the body can take up and use oxygen during intense exercise — the ceiling of aerobic power.

## Why it matters

- Sets the upper bound on sustained aerobic performance; a key trainable determinant of endurance.
- The target adaptation of high-intensity work in the top of `[[training-zones]]` (Z5).

## Key facts

- Expressed as mL O₂ · kg⁻¹ · min⁻¹ (relative) or L·min⁻¹ (absolute).
- Improved most by intensities near VO₂max — e.g. the `[[4x4-norwegian]]` interval.
- Elite Kenyan 5000 m runners measure **>80 ml·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹** (`[[src-evertsen-kenyan-physiology]]`).

## Why trained runners stop chasing it

- Trainability is finite: **VO₂max plateaus** after years of training (Paula Radcliffe held
  ~70 ml/kg/min across a decade while still getting much faster).
- Once near the ceiling, performance gains shift to **`[[running-economy]]`** and the **fraction
  of VO₂max you can hold at threshold** (`[[fractional-utilization]]`, `[[lactate-threshold]]`) —
  the rationale for the threshold-heavy `[[double-threshold]]` model. Pushing VO₂max higher needs
  costly work ≥95 % VO₂max.
